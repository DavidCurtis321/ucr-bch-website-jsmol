Lysozymes

Introduction

This script shows some aspects of the mechanism of lysozyme. Lysozyme hydrolyzes the glycosidic linkage bewteen N-acetylmuramic acid (NAM) and N-acetylglucosamine (NAG) in bacterial cell walls. (Based on 1lzc.pdb, Maenaka et al. (1995) J.Mol.Biol. 247: 281-293 and 9lyz.pdb, Kelly et al. (1979) Nature 282: 875-878)

Lysozyme 1

Lysozyme has a long cleft that will hold up to six residues of the sugar polymer (only four are shown here). The active site residues are Glu 35 and Asp 52 .

Lysozymes 2

Note how they are poised to hydrolyze the glycosidic bond between the fourth and fifth sugar residue in the polymer.

Lysozyme 3

In the transition state the NAM at residue 4 adopts a half-chair conformation. Here we see residues 2, 3, and 4 as they appear when bound to lysozyme.

Lysozymes 4

Here is the NAM at position 2. Note that it is in the chair conformation.

Lysozymes 5

Here is the NAM at position 4. Note it is in the half-chair conformation.

Lysozymes 6

The half-chair conformation is favored because of steric interactions between the C 6 group of NAM 4 and the acetamido group of NAG 3.
