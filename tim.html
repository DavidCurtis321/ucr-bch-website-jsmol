Introduction

This script shows some aspects of the mechanism of triose phosphate isomerase. (Based on 1ypi.pdb, Lolis et al. (1990) Biochemistry 29: 6609-6618 and 2ypi.pdb, Lolis & Petsko (1990) Biochemistry 29: 6619-6625)


TIM 1

Triose phosphate isomerase has an alpha/beta barrel structure with an inner barrel of beta sheets and an outer barrel of alpha helices.

Spin On/Off

TIM 2

The active site residues are Lys 12, His 95 and Glu 165, which are located on the outside of the protein.

Spin On/Off

TIM 3

In this view we see the enzyme in the absence and presence of a substrate analog . Note that only one loop (thick lines) moves when substrate binds.

Spin On/Off

TIM 4

Here is another view showing the interaction between the active site residues and the substrate. Note how the loop holds the substrate in place.

Spin On/Off

TIM 5

A closer look at the active site. Note how Lys 12 binds the phosphate of the substrate.

Spin On/Off

TIM 6

Note how Glu 165 is poised to accept a proton from either O1 or N of the substrate.

Spin On/Off

TIM 7

Note how His 95 is poised to donate a proton to either O1 or O2 of the substrate.

Spin On/Off

TIM 8

In this view we see the flexible loop over the substrate. The loop holds the substrate in place and protects the enediol intermediate from reacting with water.

Spin On/Off

TIM 9

Notice how the blue short helix is positioned relative to the substrate. The helix is essentially a dipole and, as such, its positive end stabilizes the phosphate. The yellow short helix points toward His 95. The dipole of the helix is such that its positive end orients toward the His, effectively lowering its pKa.
